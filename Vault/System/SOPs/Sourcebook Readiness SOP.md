# Sourcebook Readiness SOP

Use this SOP when developing a nation, region, settlement or other substantial part of VISCERIUM that may later support a commercial sourcebook, gazetteer, setting guide or adventure product.

## Objective

Keep canon reusable enough that a future writer can understand how a place functions without reverse-engineering it from disconnected articles.

This is **not** a completion checklist and does not imply that every subject needs book-scale detail.

> **Capture the connective tissue when it becomes useful; do not simulate the world for its own sake.**

## Sourcebook-readiness test

For a developed region or faction, a future writer should be able to recover useful answers to most of these questions from canon and its links:

- Where is it, and what other places connect to it?
- Who lives or operates there?
- What sustains ordinary life?
- Who holds authority, formally and in practice?
- What does travel into, through and out of the area involve?
- What resources, goods, services or institutions matter?
- What fauna, flora, fungi and hostile entities plausibly occur there?
- What historical events still shape present behaviour?
- What does an outsider notice or misunderstand?
- What tensions naturally create stories?

A future sourcebook writer may create presentation, tables, examples and game-system adapters later. The Codex should preserve the underlying world facts those products depend on.

## Everyday world prompts

Use these as prompts while developing important locations and factions. Answer only the questions that reveal something distinctive, consequential or repeatedly useful.

### Living here

- What do ordinary people eat, wear and live in?
- What work materially sustains households and settlements?
- How do people travel locally?
- What happens when someone is sick, injured or unable to work?
- What does childhood, training or education normally involve?
- What do people do for leisure, celebration or mourning?

### Society

- Who has authority in daily life?
- Who controls wealth, land, tools or access?
- Which laws, customs or taboos actually affect ordinary behaviour?
- Which institutions matter when people need help, permission, justice, credit, shelter or information?
- Where does social hierarchy become visible in everyday interactions?

### Material world

- What is produced locally?
- What must arrive from elsewhere?
- What is unusually cheap, common, expensive, scarce or restricted?
- Which technologies, materials or supernatural practices materially change ordinary life?
- Which goods or services make outsiders travel here?

### Movement

- Why do residents leave?
- Why do outsiders arrive?
- What does travelling a meaningful local distance require?
- Which roads, rivers, passes, ports or other routes shape movement?
- What changes with season, war, weather or Myrkild activity?

Do not document defaults simply because the prompt exists. If an ordinary assumption works and creates no setting consequence, leave it assumed.

## Location depth

Locations should grow progressively rather than beginning as exhaustive dossiers.

New location notes may record one broad `location_kind`:

- `region`
- `settlement`
- `wilderness`
- `route`
- `site`

This is deliberately broader than Atlas marker taxonomy. A fortress-city may still be a `settlement`; a mine, shrine, ruin or fort may be a `site`. Atlas markers remain responsible for map presentation.

For an existing `type: location` note, use **Templater: Insert template → Add Location Fields** when a current story or worldbuilding decision needs more structured detail.

Available modules cover:

- **Settlement** — scale, population band, authority, economic role, services and defences.
- **Wilderness** — terrain, climate, resources, travel and environmental hazards.
- **Site / ruin** — origin, condition, current use, access and notable features.
- **Route** — connected locations, ordinary traffic, conditions, seasonal change and recurring dangers.

A location may use more than one module when reality overlaps. A fortified mining settlement can legitimately need both settlement and site-like detail. Select modules because they answer a real question, not because the note looks incomplete.

## Storyteller versus world facts

Keep the layers distinct:

- **Lore and location fields** record what is true about the world.
- **Storyteller fields** record concise, actionable ways that truth affects scenes, encounters and choices.

For example, `economic_role` may establish that a town survives on ironworking. `why_people_come` may explain that travellers arrive to commission unusually durable armour. Do not duplicate the same sentence in both fields merely to fill both layers.

## When a concept becomes its own entity

Do not create a new entity type merely because a noun exists.

Promote a concept into its own canonical note when other independent pieces of canon need to reference it as a thing rather than repeatedly explain it in prose.

Strong signals include:

1. It is referenced meaningfully by several unrelated notes.
2. Its history, rules, membership, use or consequences matter independently of one parent article.
3. Updating it in one place would prevent contradictory copies elsewhere.
4. Creators need to navigate to it or establish explicit relationships with it.

This rule may eventually justify independent notes for cultures, religions, languages, professions, institutions, technologies, materials or practices. It does **not** require adding all of those as formal template types in advance.

A local funeral custom can remain inside a settlement article. A funerary tradition shared across several nations, governed by a religious institution and affecting Resonance may deserve its own note.

## Product-scoped audit

Do not measure the whole setting by percentage completion.

When testing whether an area could support a future product, define a hypothetical product scope first, for example:

> **The Okse Dominion — CITADEL regional sourcebook**

Then inspect only what that product would reasonably need. Record findings as editorial observations, not automatic tasks:

- **Strong** — enough established material already exists.
- **Thin** — material exists but a writer would need substantial invention around it.
- **Missing but valuable** — the absence would materially weaken the proposed product.
- **Safe to invent later** — presentation or gameable detail that a future product writer can create without changing canon.

Only create a task when you deliberately decide the gap should be filled now.

## First stress-test target

Use the CITADEL **Okse Dominion** as the first real sourcebook-readiness stress test because its existing article already contains unusually broad material: geography, culture, hierarchy, Resonance regulation, economy, trade, wildlife and armed forces.

The purpose is not to make Okse complete. It is to expose where a future regional writer would still have to reconstruct basic world function because the Codex failed to capture it.

After the Okse test, repeat lightly against a materially different society such as Askalia. If the same authoring model works for both without forcing irrelevant questions, the model is broad enough to keep.

## Stop rule

Stop adding structure when a future writer can make the next creative decision without inventing a foundational world fact that ought to be canonical.

More metadata is not automatically more valuable source material.
