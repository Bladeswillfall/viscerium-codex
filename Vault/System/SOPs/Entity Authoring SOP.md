# Entity Authoring SOP

Use this SOP for fauna, flora, fungi, items and similar structured creator records.

## Objective

Create enough information to place, distinguish and use a subject without turning worldbuilding into speculative administration.

## Core rule

> **Stop when you can use it.**

An entry is ready to stop when the creator can:

- identify what it is;
- distinguish it from an assumed Earth equivalent;
- place it in at least one plausible era and context;
- explain why it matters to a person, environment or story when relevant;
- use it consistently in a scene.

It does not need every optional module.

## Authoring sequence

### 1. Establish identity

Write the one-line identity first. It should answer what the subject is and why it deserves its own entry.

Weak:

> A deer-like animal found in forests.

Useful:

> A heavy forest browser whose keratin crown strips fungal bark from old trees and warns Satolian hunters of nearby Resonant disturbance.

The second version creates placement, ecology, visual identity and story use without requiring a zoological dossier.

### 2. Establish placement

Add only the eras, locations and biomes that are currently known. An empty field means undefined, not absent from canon.

Do not add `false`, `none` or exhaustive exclusion lists unless the absence itself is important and established.

### 3. Select modules deliberately

Choose an optional module only when it answers a present question.

- **Encounter or identification:** useful when a character may find, track, handle or recognise it.
- **Ecology:** useful when relationships constrain where it can exist or create consequences.
- **People or practical use:** useful when ordinary life, trade, survival or conflict interacts with it.
- **Culture:** useful when a specific people have a meaningful belief or practice around it.
- **Story seed:** useful when the subject can generate a complication beyond simply existing.

Do not select modules because a complete-looking card feels satisfying.

### 4. Prefer the distinctive over the exhaustive

Assume ordinary biological or practical functions unless Errack differs in a way that matters.

Record:

- the unusual winter carrion diet;
- the spores that reveal an old Resonant battlefield;
- the material shortage that makes an item politically important.

Usually omit:

- exact heart rate;
- exhaustive dimensions;
- reproductive timing to the day;
- manufacturing steps that do not affect availability, identity or use.

### 5. Link meaningful relationships

Prefer a useful link over another paragraph when the relationship already has its own subject.

Examples:

- consumed by `[[Species]]`;
- harvested for `[[Material]]`;
- common in `[[Location]]`;
- sacred to `[[Culture or faction]]`.

A relationship is worth recording when it helps another creator trace consequence through the setting.

## Development levels

### Stub

Use when the entry can be named, recognised and roughly placed. A stub is valid working material.

### Usable

Use when the entry can be used consistently in a scene without inventing its defining behaviour on the spot.

### Developed

Use only when narrative importance or repeated use justifies deeper ecology, culture, variants or relationships.

Do not promote an entry because it has more fields. Promote it because it has become more useful.

## Review questions

Before adding another field or paragraph, ask:

1. What decision will this information help me make?
2. Does it constrain placement, behaviour, consequence or meaning?
3. Is this fact distinctive, or am I documenting a default?
4. Does a current story need it?
5. Will maintaining it create more work than value?

If the answers are unclear, stop.
